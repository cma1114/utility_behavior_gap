# Blameless Incident Postmortem: Shared School IPs Flagged as Abusive Traffic

## Summary

On [date], students attempting to log into our service from school networks experienced widespread authentication failures. Our rate-limiting system incorrectly classified several shared school IP addresses as abusive traffic and blocked login attempts. Because many students at the affected schools appeared to originate from the same public IP, the limiter interpreted normal clustered activity as suspicious, resulting in denial of service for legitimate users.

This incident was not caused by a malicious actor. It exposed a mismatch between our traffic protections and real-world network patterns in schools, where many users share a small number of external IP addresses.

## Impact

- Student logins from affected school networks were blocked or intermittently failed.
- Users saw failed authentication or generic access-denied responses during the incident window.
- Support volume increased, and school administrators reported access problems.
- The impact was concentrated on users behind shared IPs; users on home or mobile networks were largely unaffected.
- No data loss or account compromise occurred.

## Timeline

- **09:12** — Rate limiter began classifying login bursts from a small set of IPs as abusive.
- **09:18** — First reports arrived from support indicating students could not sign in at school.
- **09:26** — On-call engineer acknowledged the alert and began investigating authentication logs and rate-limiter decisions.
- **09:34** — Pattern identified: many failed logins originated from a few IP addresses associated with school networks.
- **09:41** — Temporary mitigation applied by relaxing thresholds for login traffic on the affected IP ranges.
- **09:55** — Login success rates recovered for affected users.
- **10:20** — Additional monitoring confirmed normal authentication traffic and no continued blocking.
- **Later that day** — Follow-up review confirmed the limiter policy was too aggressive for shared-network environments.

## Contributing Factors

1. **Shared egress IPs at schools**  
   School networks often route hundreds of students through a small number of public IP addresses. Our limiter treated traffic volume per IP as a strong proxy for abuse, which is unreliable in these environments.

2. **Uniform thresholds across contexts**  
   The same rate-limiting policy was applied to all login traffic, regardless of network type, historical behavior, or known shared-network characteristics.

3. **Insufficient testing with realistic customer environments**  
   Our test data and pre-deployment validation did not include school-network patterns, so we did not observe how the limiter would behave under concentrated legitimate login traffic.

4. **Limited observability in rate-limit decisions**  
   Logs showed that requests were blocked, but the rationale was not surfaced clearly enough to quickly distinguish false positives from genuine abuse.

## Root Cause Analysis

The root cause was an overly simplistic abuse-detection rule that used per-IP request volume as a primary signal for blocking login attempts. This approach assumed that high request rates from one IP usually indicate automated abuse. That assumption fails for shared networks such as schools, libraries, and certain enterprise environments.

In this incident, the limiter lacked context about the source network and did not account for the fact that legitimate login activity can be highly concentrated behind a shared IP. The system optimized for preventing abuse at the cost of false positives, and we had not put sufficient safeguards in place to prevent legitimate users from being blocked.

## What Went Well

- Support reports and automated alerts surfaced the issue quickly.
- The on-call engineer was able to correlate failures with rate-limiter decisions within minutes.
- The team applied a targeted mitigation without disabling protections globally.
- Recovery was fast once the policy was adjusted.
- The incident prompted a clear review of assumptions in our abuse-prevention logic.

## What Went Poorly

- The rate limiter was tuned too aggressively for login traffic.
- We lacked pre-release tests that modeled shared school or institutional networks.
- The user experience during blocking was poor; affected students received generic failures with little guidance.
- We did not have sufficient guardrails to detect false-positive blocking before it affected a large user group.

## Action Items

1. **Revise rate-limiting policy for authentication traffic**  
   Incorporate additional signals beyond IP volume, such as account-level behavior and short-lived burst patterns, to reduce false positives.

2. **Create explicit exceptions for trusted shared-network environments**  
   Develop safer handling for schools, libraries, and other networks where many legitimate users share an IP.

3. **Add simulation tests for concentrated legitimate traffic**

4. **Improve rate-limit telemetry and alerting**

5. **Add user-facing guidance for blocked logins**

6. **Review existing thresholds on all high-value user flows**

This incident highlighted the need to design abuse-prevention systems around real user environments, not just idealized traffic models. We will use these findings to make authentication protections safer and more adaptive.
