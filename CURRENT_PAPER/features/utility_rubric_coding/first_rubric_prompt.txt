## Summary

On [date], our authentication rate limiter incorrectly classified traffic from shared school network IP addresses as abusive and blocked student login attempts. This caused a widespread login outage for students accessing the platform from school networks. The issue persisted until we identified the pattern, adjusted the blocking logic, and deployed a mitigation.

This incident was not caused by malicious activity or operator error. It resulted from a mismatch between our abuse-prevention assumptions and the realities of shared-network environments.

## Impact

Students on affected school networks were unable to log in for approximately [duration]. The impact was concentrated in schools that route many users through a small number of public IP addresses. Because many legitimate users appeared to originate from the same IP, the rate limiter triggered on normal login bursts.

Observed impact included:
- Failed login attempts for students behind shared school IPs
- Increased support contacts from schools and parents

- Elevated authentication error rates in monitoring and logs

The issue did not affect all users, but it disproportionately impacted school-based access during peak login times.

- **08:10** — Authentication error rate begins to rise for a subset of users.
- **08:25** — Support receives first report from a school that students cannot sign in.
- **08:40** — On-call investigates login failures and sees rate-limit responses clustered around a small set of IP addresses.

- **09:40** — Login access restored for most affected users.

- **Same day** — Longer-term fix designed and scheduled for rollout.
- **[Later date]** — Permanent changes deployed and monitored.

## Contributing Factors

1. **Shared IP behavior in schools**
   - Hundreds of legitimate users may appear under one or a few public IPs due to NAT or proxying.

   - The limiter relied too heavily on request rate per IP and insufficiently on per-account and behavioral signals.

3. **Peak-time login bursts**
   - School logins often cluster around class start times, creating legitimate spikes that resembled abuse.

4. **Insufficient environment coverage in testing**
   - Our validation did not include realistic school-network simulations or traffic patterns.

5. **Alerting that detected symptoms, not cause**

The root cause was an abuse-detection rule that assumed high login volume from a single IP indicated abusive behavior. That assumption fails in environments where many legitimate users share a public IP address, such as schools.

- The rate limiter applied a threshold at the IP level.
- It did not sufficiently weight account-specific behavior, device signals, or network context.
- It lacked an allowlist or adaptive policy for known shared-network environments.
- As a result, normal student login bursts were misclassified as abuse and blocked.

In short, the system optimized for protecting against brute-force or bot traffic but did not account for legitimate high-density usage from shared networks.

- On-call identified the issue quickly once support escalated from an affected school.
- Logs clearly showed the common IP pattern, which helped isolate the rate limiter as the source.

- The incident was handled without manual workarounds that would have created security exceptions or ad hoc bypasses.
- Teams collaborated effectively across support, engineering, and operations to diagnose and resolve the issue.

## What Went Poorly

- We did not anticipate that a standard school network would look abusive to our current heuristic.
- The rate limiter’s assumptions were not documented or reviewed against real-world education deployments.
- We lacked a safe fallback path for shared-network traffic.
- The first alert was user-facing login failures rather than a direct signal that the rate limiter was overblocking legitimate users.
- We had no pre-built simulation or test fixture for high-density NAT scenarios.

This incident was caused by an abuse-prevention control that was too narrow for the environments we serve. The corrective work focuses on making the login protection system more context-aware while preserving security. Our goal is to prevent a recurrence without weakening defenses against actual abuse.
